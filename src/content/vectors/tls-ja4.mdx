> **TL;DR.** Every TLS client (browser, OS, library) negotiates with a slightly different list of cipher suites, extensions, and curves in a slightly different order. Servers hash that order. The hash identifies the client, often down to a specific browser build on a specific OS.

JA4 is the 2023 successor to JA3. It splits TCP, QUIC, and HTTP fingerprints, includes ALPN and signature algorithms, and is harder to spoof than JA3 was.

## How it works

When your browser opens a connection, the first packet (the ClientHello) lists everything your client supports: TLS versions, cipher suites, extensions, supported groups, signature algorithms, ALPN protocols. Most clients send these in a stable order. Cloudflare, Akamai, Imperva, and most bot-management vendors record JA3, JA4, and JA4_r and use them as a primary signal for "is this really Chrome on macOS, or a Python script lying about its User-Agent?"

A spoofed User-Agent without a matching JA4 is a red flag. So is an outdated JA4 from a fully-patched browser version string.

## What it leaks about you

Your real client family and version, your OS family, and whether you are using a custom HTTP library, headless browser, or scraping framework.

## How to defend

- 🟢 Use a mainstream browser version. The crowd is the cover.
- 🟡 If you must script, use a stack that mimics a real browser's TLS stack (curl-impersonate, undetected-chromedriver).
- 🔴 Tor Browser standardizes its TLS stack across all users by design.

## What doesn't help

User-Agent spoofing. The TLS layer happens before any HTTP header is sent.
