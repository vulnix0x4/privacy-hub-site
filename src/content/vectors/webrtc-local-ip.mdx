> **TL;DR.** WebRTC, the browser API for video calls, exposes your local network IP (10.x.x.x or 192.168.x.x) and sometimes your real public IP through a side channel called STUN. A VPN does not always block it.

The original sin was design: WebRTC needs to know your local IP to set up direct peer connections, and it does so with no permission prompt.

## How it works

Any script can ask the browser for an `RTCPeerConnection`, then read the ICE candidates the browser generates. Those candidates list every IP the browser thinks could route a call. That includes your LAN IP and, in older or misconfigured stacks, your real public IP behind the VPN tunnel.

Combined with a public IP, the LAN IP narrows you to a specific household: the same /24 plus the same NAT lease pattern is a strong join across cleared cookies.

## What it leaks about you

Your local subnet (and from it, your router model in many cases). Sometimes your real ISP-assigned IP even while a VPN is connected.

## How to defend

- 🟢 In Firefox, set `media.peerconnection.enabled` to `false` if you do not use video calls in the browser.
- 🟢 In Brave, Settings -&gt; Privacy and security -&gt; WebRTC IP handling policy -&gt; "Disable Non-Proxied UDP".
- 🟡 Use a privacy extension that blocks WebRTC IP leaks (uBlock Origin has the toggle in advanced mode).
- 🔴 Block UDP at the firewall outside your VPN tunnel.

## What doesn't help

A VPN alone, if your browser exposes a non-proxied candidate. Always test after enabling the VPN.
