> **TL;DR.** Your clients handed you access. A client-data leak ends your business faster than a missed deadline. A tiny one-person security program — encrypted disk, password manager, hardware 2FA, audited VPN, zero-knowledge file storage — covers the 95th percentile of bad outcomes for the cost of one afternoon setup.

## Threat picture

The realistic threats: a lost laptop at a coffee shop, a phishing email that steals a client SSH key, a backup drive thrown in the trash by a roommate, and a subpoena to a cloud provider who had your client files in plaintext. Less realistic but possible: a targeted attack on you specifically because a client is high-profile.

## What you get from this playlist

A defensible one-person shop. You can fill out a vendor-security questionnaire honestly: encrypted disk, unique credentials per client, hardware 2FA, encrypted backups, provider-inaccessible file storage. Nothing exotic; all of it meaningful if something goes wrong.

## Notes from real people

Freelancers who take this seriously say the single biggest mistake is using personal accounts (personal Dropbox, personal Gmail) for client files. Buy a business email, use a business password manager organization, separate the bookkeeping. When a client asks for your security posture, you have real answers.

## What we don't cover here

Formal compliance programs (SOC 2, ISO 27001, HIPAA). Those require auditors, documented policies, and a tax bill. If your clients start asking for certifications, you have outgrown a one-person ops model; Vanta and Drata are the usual starting points.
