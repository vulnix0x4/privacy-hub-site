> **TL;DR.** The goal is autonomy, not surveillance. The spy-on-your-kid apps are largely ineffective, often insecure themselves, and they model exactly the wrong lesson about privacy. Teach habits; install tools once; trust your kid to grow into it.

## Threat picture

Your actual threats in order: data-broker aggregation of your kid's information, ad-driven attention extraction, school-district software selling their reading scores, and eventually whatever platform they sign up for at 13. Predators are the cover story; commercial surveillance is the actual footprint.

## What you get from this playlist

A household where the adults use good privacy tools visibly, the kids inherit the defaults, and nobody is on Google Family Link as the primary trust layer. Age-appropriate conversations about what "agreeing to terms" actually means.

## Notes from real people

Parents who pulled this off say the consistent thread is not installing anything the adult does not use themselves. If you are still on Gmail while the kid uses Proton, the kid learns that privacy is what you tell them to do.

## What we don't cover here

Content moderation. Whether a twelve-year-old should be on TikTok is not a privacy question. The American Academy of Pediatrics and Common Sense Media have usable frameworks; Marc Faddoul's work at Tracking Exposed covers the algorithmic-harm side.
