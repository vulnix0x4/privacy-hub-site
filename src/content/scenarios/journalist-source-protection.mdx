> **TL;DR.** Your source took a real risk. The tools that protect them are the same tools that protect you when the legal department starts asking questions. Compartmentalize devices, use SecureDrop if the outlet runs one, and treat every piece of metadata as attackable.

## Threat picture

Depends on the story. Could be a corporate legal team with subpoena power, could be a state actor with signals-intelligence budget, could be a dedicated internal-investigations unit at the source's employer. In every case: the source's phone records, the source's work email, the source's badge logs, and your own movements around the time of contact are the first places an investigator looks. The goal is to ensure none of those signals intersect.

## What you get from this playlist

A hardened contact and review pipeline. Signal-with-disappearing-messages as the default channel, a per-story alias, an encrypted review vault. Not a SecureDrop substitute. If your outlet has one, use it.

## Notes from real people

Every investigative reporter will tell you: the source protection plan is decided in the first conversation. If you let the source DM you on Twitter once, that link is now in Twitter's database forever. Steer every first contact to Signal within the first reply.

## What we don't cover here

Legal strategy, pre-publication shield-law invocation, and outlet-level workflows. Freedom of the Press Foundation (https://freedom.press) is the canonical resource; their threat-modeling training has kept many sources out of jail.
