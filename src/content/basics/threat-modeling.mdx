> **TL;DR.** A threat model is a sentence of the form: *these are the things I want to protect, from these people, and I am willing to do this much work.* You write it once, re-read it when a new tool tempts you, and most of the bad decisions cancel themselves.

## The six questions

Answer in order. Your answers are allowed to be boring.

1. **What do I actually want to keep private?** Messages, photos, browsing history, your home address, where you were last Tuesday. Pick the top three.
2. **Who do I want to keep those things from?** Advertisers, your employer, an ex, a specific government, a specific family member. Name them. "Hackers" is not an answer. Name the actual adversary.
3. **How motivated are they?** Casual scraping is one bar. A person who knows your full name and is actively looking is a very different bar. Most people are in the first bucket.
4. **What happens if they succeed?** Embarrassment, money, physical safety, job loss. The worst case determines how much effort the rest is worth.
5. **How much work am I willing to do?** Fifteen minutes a month, an hour a weekend, or a full lifestyle rebuild. Be honest. The plan you won't do is worse than the plan you will.
6. **What can I not change?** Using Slack for work. iMessage because everyone you love is on it. These are constraints, not failures.

## What to do with the answers

Copy them into a note somewhere (Obsidian, Apple Notes, whatever). When a new tool or guide tempts you, check whether it addresses anything in questions 1–4, and whether the work in question 5 still fits. If both, do it. If either is a no, skip it.

That is the whole exercise. EFF's Surveillance Self-Defense has a longer version with worksheets if this one felt too quick. It is not wrong to want more structure.

## What a threat model is not

It is not an excuse to ignore things outside of it. ("Passwords are not in my model, therefore I reuse them" is not a valid conclusion.) Treat it as a filter against tool-bloat, not as permission to skip fundamentals.
